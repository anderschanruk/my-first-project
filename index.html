Definition

What Are Cardiometabolic Diseases?

Cardiometabolic diseases are a cluster of interconnected conditions that arise from disruptions to metabolism and cardiovascular function — often sharing the same root causes.

Cardiovascular Disease

A group of disorders affecting the heart and blood vessels, including coronary artery disease, heart failure, stroke, and peripheral arterial disease. Metabolic disturbances dramatically accelerate cardiovascular damage.

Type 2 Diabetes

A chronic condition in which cells become resistant to insulin, causing elevated blood glucose. Strongly driven by excess visceral fat, inactivity, and chronic inflammation — and a major driver of cardiovascular risk.

Obesity

Excess body fat — especially around the abdomen — creates a state of low-grade chronic inflammation and hormonal disruption that underpins nearly every other cardiometabolic condition.

Metabolic Syndrome

A combination of high blood pressure, elevated triglycerides, low HDL cholesterol, excess waist circumference, and elevated fasting glucose. Having three or more of these multiplies cardiovascular risk.

MASLD / Fatty Liver Disease

Metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease (MASLD) affects around 25% of adults worldwide and frequently co-occurs with diabetes and cardiovascular disease, worsening outcomes across all three.

Hypertension

Chronically elevated blood pressure is both a cause and consequence of cardiometabolic dysfunction. It silently damages the heart, kidneys, brain, and arteries for years before causing an acute event.

The Cardiometabolic Connection

These conditions do not exist in isolation. Obesity drives insulin resistance, which drives type 2 diabetes. Diabetes accelerates atherosclerosis, which drives cardiovascular events. Hypertension worsens kidney disease. Fatty liver amplifies all of the above. Understanding this web of interactions is essential to effective prevention and treatment.

Global Burden

A Mounting Crisis

Cardiometabolic diseases are now the leading cause of premature death and disability worldwide — and the numbers are accelerating.

540M
people live with diabetes globally (IDF 2021), with 90% having type 2 diabetes
1.28B
adults worldwide have hypertension — two-thirds are in low- and middle-income countries
2.5B
adults are overweight or obese globally as of 2022, a figure that has tripled since 1975
17.9M
deaths are caused by cardiovascular disease each year — 32% of all global deaths

Why the Numbers Are Getting Worse

↗
Urbanisation & sedentary lifestyles

More than 55% of the global population now lives in urban areas. Office work, screen time, and car dependency have dramatically reduced daily physical activity across all age groups.

↗
Ultra-processed food environments

Ultra-processed foods now represent 56% of caloric intake in high-income countries. They are engineered to drive overconsumption, displace whole foods, and promote the exact metabolic disruptions that cause these diseases.

↗
Ageing populations

The world's population aged 60+ will double by 2050. Older age is strongly associated with declining metabolic function, making the cardiometabolic burden inevitable without early intervention.

↗
Health inequity

Low- and middle-income countries bear 80% of the global mortality from cardiometabolic disease, yet receive a fraction of global health funding. Structural disadvantage drives excess risk at every level.

↗
Chronic stress & sleep deprivation

Modern work patterns and social pressures elevate cortisol chronically. Poor sleep independently raises blood glucose, promotes visceral fat accumulation, and increases cardiovascular risk.

Key Facts & Data

What the Science Tells Us

Decades of research have established clear links between biological, behavioural, and societal factors in the development of cardiometabolic disease.

80%

of type 2 diabetes cases and a large proportion of cardiovascular events are considered preventable through lifestyle modification — diet, physical activity, and smoking cessation.

70%

of people with type 2 diabetes will develop cardiovascular disease during their lifetime. The two conditions share deep biological roots in insulin resistance and chronic inflammation.

5–10%

body weight reduction in overweight individuals significantly reduces blood pressure, blood glucose, and LDL cholesterol — often achieving clinical improvements comparable to medication.

500M

people currently have prediabetes and most are unaware of it. Without intervention, around 70% will progress to full type 2 diabetes within a decade.

The Role of Visceral Fat

Not all body fat is equal. Visceral fat — stored around the abdominal organs — is metabolically active, secreting inflammatory cytokines and free fatty acids that disrupt insulin signalling, elevate triglycerides, reduce HDL cholesterol, and damage arterial walls. Waist circumference is a stronger predictor of cardiometabolic risk than BMI alone.

Chronic Inflammation as the Common Thread

Low-grade systemic inflammation links obesity, diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and liver disease. Elevated biomarkers like hs-CRP, IL-6, and TNF-α are found across all cardiometabolic conditions. Anti-inflammatory dietary patterns and physical activity consistently reduce these markers and the risk of disease progression.

The Gut Microbiome Connection

Emerging evidence implicates the gut microbiome as a key regulator of metabolic health. Disrupted microbiome composition (dysbiosis) is associated with insulin resistance, obesity, and cardiovascular risk. Diet — particularly fibre and fermented foods — is the most modifiable determinant of microbiome health.

Early-Onset Trends

Young-onset cardiometabolic disease is accelerating. Type 2 diabetes in under-40s has more than doubled in the past two decades. Young people with early-onset disease face longer disease duration and, consequently, greater cumulative risk of complications. Paediatric obesity rates make this a multi-generational challenge.

Prevention

What Can Be Done

The evidence base for prevention is robust. Both individual actions and structural change at the population level are needed to turn the tide.

01

Prioritise Dietary Quality

A diet rich in vegetables, legumes, whole grains, nuts, and oily fish — and low in ultra-processed foods, refined carbohydrates, and added sugar — consistently reduces cardiometabolic risk across all populations. No single diet is optimal; the overall pattern matters most.

Strong evidence (RCTs & cohort studies)
02

Move More, Sit Less

150–300 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week, combined with muscle-strengthening activities twice weekly, is the global standard. Even breaking up prolonged sitting with brief movement bouts measurably improves glucose metabolism.

Strong evidence
03

Achieve and Maintain a Healthy Weight

For individuals with overweight or obesity, even modest sustained weight loss (5–7%) substantially reduces the incidence of type 2 diabetes, improves blood pressure, and lowers cardiovascular risk. Behavioural support and, where indicated, medical therapy can support this.

Strong evidence (Diabetes Prevention Program)
04

Protect Your Sleep

Seven to nine hours of quality sleep per night supports healthy glucose metabolism, maintains hormonal balance, and reduces appetite-regulating hormone disruption. Shift work and chronic sleep restriction independently raise cardiometabolic risk.

Emerging strong evidence
05

Manage Chronic Stress

Chronic psychological stress elevates cortisol, which raises blood glucose, promotes visceral fat accumulation, and increases cardiovascular risk. Mindfulness-based stress reduction, regular physical activity, and social connection are evidence-based approaches.

Moderate-strong evidence
06

Avoid Tobacco and Limit Alcohol

Smoking is an independent risk factor for cardiovascular disease and type 2 diabetes. Excessive alcohol consumption raises blood pressure, contributes to fatty liver disease, and disrupts metabolic health. No level of smoking is safe.

Strong evidence
01

Screen Early and Often

Cardiometabolic diseases develop silently. Regular screening for blood glucose, blood pressure, lipids, and waist circumference — starting from age 35–40, or earlier with risk factors — enables identification of prediabetes and early cardiovascular risk before symptoms emerge.

WHO & IDF guidelines
02

Structured Prevention Programmes

The Finnish Diabetes Prevention Study and the US Diabetes Prevention Program demonstrated that intensive lifestyle intervention in high-risk individuals reduces type 2 diabetes incidence by 58% — outperforming metformin alone. Scalable digital and group-based versions exist.

Level 1 evidence (RCTs)
03

Treat-to-Target Approaches

For those already diagnosed, simultaneously managing blood glucose, blood pressure, and LDL cholesterol to evidence-based targets dramatically reduces the risk of complications. Multifactorial risk treatment in the Steno-2 trial cut all-cause mortality by 50%.

Steno-2 & ACCORD trials
04

New Therapeutic Options

GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide, liraglutide) and SGLT-2 inhibitors now have proven cardiovascular and kidney protection benefits beyond glucose control. Cardiometabolic medicine is rapidly evolving toward disease modification, not just symptom management.

LEADER, SUSTAIN, EMPA-REG trials
01

Food System Reform

Taxation of sugar-sweetened beverages, reformulation of processed foods, front-of-pack nutrition labelling, and restrictions on unhealthy food marketing to children are structural interventions with strong evidence of population-level impact.

WCRF & WHO recommendations
02

Built Environment & Active Design

Urban planning that prioritises walking, cycling, green spaces, and access to affordable healthy food reduces cardiometabolic risk at the population level. The built environment is one of the strongest determinants of physical activity levels.

Strong population evidence
03

Health Equity as Prevention

Addressing socioeconomic determinants of health — income inequality, food insecurity, occupational stress, and limited healthcare access — is essential. Cardiometabolic risk is not equally distributed; targeted prevention must reach those at greatest disadvantage.

Social determinants framework (WHO)
04

Predictive Population Health

AI and machine learning models applied to health registry data can identify individuals at highest risk years before clinical diagnosis, enabling proactive outreach. Research funded by the Novo Nordisk Foundation has demonstrated 100x improved prediction accuracy for type 2 diabetes using 20 years of Danish health registry data.

NNF-funded research, Lancet Digital Health (pending)
