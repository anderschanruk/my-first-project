Definition

What Are Cardiometabolic
Diseases?

A cluster of interconnected conditions arising from disruptions to metabolism and cardiovascular function — often sharing the same root causes and amplifying each other.

Cardiovascular Disease

Disorders affecting the heart and blood vessels — coronary artery disease, heart failure, stroke, and peripheral arterial disease. Metabolic disturbances dramatically accelerate cardiovascular damage.

Type 2 Diabetes

Cells become resistant to insulin, causing elevated blood glucose. Strongly driven by excess visceral fat, inactivity, and chronic inflammation — and a major driver of cardiovascular risk.

Obesity

Excess body fat — especially abdominal — creates low-grade chronic inflammation and hormonal disruption that underpins nearly every other cardiometabolic condition.

Metabolic Syndrome

High blood pressure, elevated triglycerides, low HDL, excess waist circumference, and elevated fasting glucose. Three or more of these dramatically multiplies cardiovascular risk.

MASLD / Fatty Liver

Metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease affects ~25% of adults and frequently co-occurs with diabetes and cardiovascular disease, worsening outcomes across all three.

Hypertension

Chronically elevated blood pressure silently damages the heart, kidneys, brain, and arteries for years before causing an acute event. Both cause and consequence of cardiometabolic dysfunction.

The Cardiometabolic Connection

These conditions don't exist in isolation. Obesity drives insulin resistance → which drives type 2 diabetes → which accelerates atherosclerosis → which drives cardiovascular events. Hypertension worsens kidney disease. Fatty liver amplifies all of the above. Understanding this web of interactions is essential to effective prevention and treatment.

"Cardiometabolic disease is not inevitable. It is the predictable consequence of environments and behaviours that can be changed."
Global Burden

A Mounting Crisis

Cardiometabolic diseases are the leading cause of premature death and disability worldwide — and the numbers are accelerating.

589M

adults live with diabetes globally — over 90% type 2, and ~252M remain undiagnosed

1.28B

adults have hypertension — two-thirds in low- and middle-income countries

2.5B

adults are overweight (2022) — and the prevalence of obesity has more than doubled since 1990

17.9M

cardiovascular deaths per year — 32% of all global mortality

Why the numbers are getting worse

01
Urbanisation & sedentary lifestyles

More than 55% of the global population now lives in urban areas. Office work, screen time, and car dependency have dramatically reduced daily physical activity across all age groups.

02
Ultra-processed food environments

Ultra-processed foods represent 56% of caloric intake in high-income countries — engineered to drive overconsumption and promote the exact metabolic disruptions that cause these diseases.

03
Ageing populations

The world's population aged 60+ will double by 2050. Older age is strongly associated with declining metabolic function, making the burden inevitable without early intervention.

04
Health inequity

Low- and middle-income countries bear 80% of global cardiometabolic mortality, yet receive a fraction of global health funding. Structural disadvantage drives excess risk at every level.

05
Chronic stress & sleep deprivation

Modern work patterns elevate cortisol chronically. Poor sleep independently raises blood glucose, promotes visceral fat accumulation, and increases cardiovascular risk.

Key Facts & Data

What the Science Tells Us

Decades of research have established clear links between biological, behavioural, and societal factors in cardiometabolic disease.

80%

of type 2 diabetes cases and a large proportion of cardiovascular events are considered preventable through lifestyle modification.

70%

of people with type 2 diabetes will develop cardiovascular disease during their lifetime — both share roots in insulin resistance.

5–10%

body weight reduction significantly reduces blood pressure, blood glucose, and LDL cholesterol — often comparable to medication.

500M

people currently have prediabetes and most are unaware. Without intervention, ~70% will progress to type 2 diabetes within a decade.

The Role of Visceral Fat

Not all fat is equal. Visceral fat secretes inflammatory cytokines and free fatty acids that disrupt insulin signalling, elevate triglycerides, and damage arterial walls. Waist circumference is a stronger predictor of cardiometabolic risk than BMI alone.

Chronic Inflammation

Low-grade systemic inflammation links obesity, diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and liver disease. Elevated biomarkers like hs-CRP and IL-6 are found across all conditions. Anti-inflammatory diets and physical activity consistently reduce these markers.

The Gut Microbiome

Disrupted microbiome composition is associated with insulin resistance, obesity, and cardiovascular risk. Diet — particularly fibre and fermented foods — is the most modifiable determinant of microbiome health.

Early-Onset Trends

Type 2 diabetes in under-40s has more than doubled in two decades. Young people with early-onset disease face longer disease duration and greater cumulative complications risk. Paediatric obesity makes this a multi-generational challenge.

Prevention

What Can Be Done

The evidence base for prevention is robust. Both individual actions and structural change at the population level are needed to turn the tide.

01

Prioritise Dietary Quality

A diet rich in vegetables, legumes, whole grains, nuts, and oily fish — low in ultra-processed foods — consistently reduces cardiometabolic risk across all populations.

Strong evidence (RCTs & cohorts)
02

Move More, Sit Less

150–300 minutes of moderate aerobic activity plus strength training twice weekly. Even breaking up prolonged sitting measurably improves glucose metabolism.

Strong evidence
03

Maintain a Healthy Weight

Even modest sustained weight loss (5–7%) substantially reduces type 2 diabetes incidence, improves blood pressure, and lowers cardiovascular risk.

Diabetes Prevention Program
04

Protect Your Sleep

7–9 hours of quality sleep supports healthy glucose metabolism and hormonal balance. Chronic sleep restriction independently raises cardiometabolic risk.

Emerging strong evidence
05

Manage Chronic Stress

Chronic stress elevates cortisol, raises blood glucose, and promotes visceral fat. Mindfulness, physical activity, and social connection are evidence-based approaches.

Moderate-strong evidence
06

Avoid Tobacco & Limit Alcohol

Smoking is an independent risk factor for cardiovascular disease and type 2 diabetes. Excessive alcohol raises blood pressure and drives fatty liver disease.

Strong evidence
01

Screen Early and Often

Regular screening for blood glucose, blood pressure, lipids, and waist circumference from age 35–40 enables identification of prediabetes years before symptoms emerge.

WHO & IDF guidelines
02

Structured Prevention Programmes

Intensive lifestyle intervention in high-risk individuals reduces type 2 diabetes incidence by 58% — outperforming metformin alone. Scalable digital and group-based versions now exist.

Level 1 evidence (RCTs)
03

Treat-to-Target Approaches

Simultaneously managing blood glucose, blood pressure, and LDL to evidence-based targets dramatically reduces complications. Multifactorial treatment in Steno-2 cut all-cause mortality by 50%.

Steno-2 & ACCORD trials
04

New Therapeutic Options

GLP-1 receptor agonists and SGLT-2 inhibitors now have proven cardiovascular and kidney protection beyond glucose control — shifting medicine toward disease modification.

LEADER, SUSTAIN, EMPA-REG
01

Food System Reform

Taxation of sugar-sweetened beverages, reformulation of processed foods, front-of-pack labelling, and restrictions on marketing to children — all with strong population-level evidence.

WCRF & WHO recommendations
02

Built Environment & Active Design

Urban planning prioritising walking, cycling, green spaces, and access to affordable healthy food reduces cardiometabolic risk across entire populations.

Strong population evidence
03

Health Equity as Prevention

Addressing income inequality, food insecurity, and limited healthcare access is essential. Cardiometabolic risk is not equally distributed — targeted prevention must reach those at greatest disadvantage.

Social determinants (WHO)
04

Predictive Population Health

AI applied to health registry data can identify individuals at highest risk years before diagnosis. NNF-funded research demonstrated 100× improved prediction accuracy using 20 years of Danish health data.

NNF research — Lancet Digital Health
